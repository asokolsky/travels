# Touring NVIDIA HQ

Assumption: this is either a hosted visit to [NVIDIA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia)'s [Santa Clara, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California) headquarters or an exterior architecture stop. As of May 17, 2026, I found official visitor logistics for registered visitors, but not an official public-tour program; treat interior access as invite-only unless a host confirms otherwise. [[1]](#source-1)[[2]](#source-2)

## Quick Facts

- Corporate address: NVIDIA Corporate, 2788 San Tomas Expressway, Santa Clara, CA 95051. [[1]](#source-1)
- Main public-facing campus landmarks: Endeavor and Voyager, the two large triangular/low-rise headquarters buildings west of San Tomas Expressway, plus the landscaped connector park between them. [[2]](#source-2)[[3]](#source-3)[[4]](#source-4)
- Architect: [Gensler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gensler), with Devcon Construction listed as general contractor on multiple project sources. [[3]](#source-3)[[5]](#source-5)[[6]](#source-6)
- Scale: the campus is about 1.25 million square feet, generally described as 500,000 square feet for Endeavor plus 750,000 square feet for Voyager. [[3]](#source-3)[[5]](#source-5)[[7]](#source-7)
- Name/culture note: the Endeavor and Voyager names fit the campus's space/sci-fi language; tech coverage has explicitly linked the pair to Star Trek starships. [[14]](#source-14)
- Sustainability: Gensler lists the campus as LEED Gold certified, with a 390 kW solar panel array, reclaimed water for irrigation, and outdoor workplace capacity for about 1,300 occupants. NVIDIA's FY2024 sustainability report says the Santa Clara Voyager and Endeavor buildings are LEED Gold certified and connected by a three-acre park shaded by a trellis with solar panels. [[3]](#source-3)[[8]](#source-8)
- Best mental model: this is not a museum campus like the Intel Museum. It is an active corporate headquarters with reception, visitor badges, shuttles, employee workspaces, labs, and security boundaries. [[2]](#source-2)

## Visitor Logistics

- Visitor parking: NVIDIA's current visitor map points visitors to Building S at 2330 Central Expressway, Santa Clara, CA 95050, unless the host gives different instructions. [[2]](#source-2)
- Check-in: the official visitor map says all visitors must be registered to access campus; on-site registration/check-in is at Building S, and reception is listed as available daily from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. [[2]](#source-2)
- Endeavor access: the visitor map says there is no visitor parking at Endeavor. It directs visitors either to park at Building S and take the parking shuttle to Endeavor or, for one entrance, use the footbridge facing the Walsh and San Tomas intersection. [[2]](#source-2)
- Voyager access: the visitor map says there is no visitor parking at Voyager. Park at Building S and take the parking shuttle unless a host directs otherwise. [[2]](#source-2)
- Shuttle: visitors must present a QR code or visitor badge to board the shuttle, which stops at Endeavor, Voyager, and Building S. [[2]](#source-2)
- Rideshare: official rideshare stops may be used only for loading and unloading, not parking. [[2]](#source-2)
- EV charging: guest EV charging is not available, per the visitor map. [[2]](#source-2)
- Practical advice: bring government-issued ID, arrive early enough for badge logistics, and ask the host before taking photos inside. The exterior is photogenic, but the site is still a working R&D campus.

## Architecture Lens

- The big design idea is a workplace organized around connection. Gensler says the campus takes cues from chip design: large open floor plates, visible staircases, collaborative platforms, outdoor workspaces, and a network-like layout meant to reduce departmental silos. [[3]](#source-3)
- The buildings' triangular geometry is intentional. Gensler calls the triangular campus one of Silicon Valley's recognizable headquarters, and contemporary architecture coverage described the triangular forms as a nod to the fundamental building block of computer graphics. [[3]](#source-3)[[9]](#source-9)
- Endeavor is the earlier 500,000-square-foot phase, completed in 2017. It was planned as an office-and-lab building with two office/lab levels and two parking levels, and its roof design used triangular skylights to bring daylight into deep floor plates. [[5]](#source-5)[[9]](#source-9)
- Voyager is the 750,000-square-foot second phase, completed/opened around 2022. It extends the same campus language but is larger, more biophilic, and more landscape-driven. [[5]](#source-5)[[6]](#source-6)[[7]](#source-7)
- Voyager's envelope is a major part of the architectural effect: Enclos describes a sloped glass curtain-wall perimeter, abundant daylight, large views, interior planting, and double-paned insulated glass units designed to reduce heat gain, glare, and shadow. [[6]](#source-6)
- Voyager's structure is also worth noticing. IMEG describes the project as a three-story office building with a conference center, video recording studio, mezzanine levels, two underground parking levels, structural steel with concrete-filled metal deck floors, a vaulted/faceted roof, 260 triangular skylights, a central atrium with a 90-foot span, raised floors, radiant heating/cooling, and extensive terrazzo flooring. [[7]](#source-7)
- The landscape is not filler. NVIDIA's sustainability report says Endeavor and Voyager are linked by a three-acre park shaded by a solar trellis; Sares Regis describes a private outdoor workspace and biophilic design as central to the campus. [[5]](#source-5)[[8]](#source-8)
- The design is trying to make work feel less like sealed office space. Gensler and Architizer both frame Voyager around indoor-outdoor connection, natural light, and the idea that people do better creative work when they can move among different kinds of spaces. [[3]](#source-3)[[10]](#source-10)

## What to Look For

- From the outside, look first at the roof geometry. The buildings read best from an elevated or diagonal angle, but even from ground level the faceted rooflines and low, broad massing make the campus feel more like a sci-fi pavilion than a standard office block.
- Notice how the campus avoids a single tower. The headquarters spreads horizontally, using large floor plates, atria, bridges, stairs, and garden space to create chance encounters.
- In Endeavor, watch for the stronger triangle motif: roof modules, skylights, and sharp interior/exterior geometries.
- In Voyager, watch for softer biophilic cues: more planting, larger glazed edges, views into green space, daylight as the main interior experience, and the big atrium volume.
- In the connector park, look for the solar trellis and the way the open space stitches the two buildings together. This is one of the best places to understand the campus as a single composition rather than two unrelated buildings.
- If your host can take you inside, ask to see the main atrium/heart area, visible staircases, informal collaboration platforms, and any display walls or hardware history areas that are guest-appropriate.
- If you are only doing an exterior stop, pair the drive-by with the NVIDIA Newsroom photo gallery before or after the visit; its aerial and interior images make the design logic much easier to understand. [[4]](#source-4)

## Suggested Hosted Visit Flow

1. Check in at Building S and confirm the exact route with reception or your host. Do not assume you can park at Endeavor or Voyager. [[2]](#source-2)
2. Start outside with the campus composition: Endeavor, Voyager, the connector park, solar trellis, and the San Tomas/Walsh edge. This gives context before the interior.
3. Walk into the assigned lobby or atrium and read the building from the inside out: daylight, roof geometry, stairs, work platforms, and sightlines.
4. Spend a few minutes on the architecture story: triangle as graphics primitive, chip-design-inspired networks, and the deliberate refusal of a generic office-box plan. [[3]](#source-3)[[9]](#source-9)
5. Ask about how work happens there now: how labs, confidential engineering, open collaboration, events, and hybrid work fit inside one campus.
6. End with the landscape and sustainability layer: reclaimed water, solar trellis, LEED Gold, shade, and why the outdoor work zones matter in the Bay Area climate. [[3]](#source-3)[[8]](#source-8)

## Questions to Ask a Host

- How much of the daily work is done in Endeavor versus Voyager?
- Are the buildings organized by function, product group, or collaboration pattern?
- How do confidential labs coexist with open atria and public-facing visitor paths?
- How has hybrid work changed the way Voyager is used since opening?
- What design choices have worked best in practice: the staircases, outdoor workspaces, atria, daylight, or team zones?
- Did NVIDIA tools such as Iray, Omniverse, or GPU rendering influence design review, daylight studies, or material decisions? Sares Regis says NVIDIA Iray was used for material selection and daylight/interior-light studies. [[5]](#source-5)
- How does the campus handle heat, glare, and acoustic control under so much glass and roof volume?
- What is the one part of the campus most guests miss but architects notice?

## Nearby Context

- NVIDIA's origin story is nearby but not on the HQ campus. NVIDIA's official history says the company was founded on April 5, 1993, by Jensen Huang, Chris Malachowsky, and Curtis Priem, with a vision to bring 3D graphics to gaming and multimedia markets. [[11]](#source-11)
- NVIDIA's own blog says the founders developed the idea for the company at a Denny's in East San Jose, and Jensen Huang returned there in 2023 for a plaque event. If you are making a Silicon Valley tech-history loop, that Denny's is the more human counterpoint to the polished HQ architecture. [[12]](#source-12)
- If you want an actual public tech museum in the same general area, the [Intel Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_Museum) is the better public-facing companion stop; Intel currently lists weekday public hours and advises calling ahead because the museum can close for special events. NVIDIA HQ itself should be treated as hosted corporate access or exterior architecture viewing. [[13]](#source-13)
