For a U.S. taxpayer living in the European Union, two different legal systems can
make ordinary fund investing awkward:

- U.S. **PFIC** rules can impose unfavorable tax treatment and annual reporting
  on shares of a foreign corporation that meets either the 75% passive-income
  test or the 50% passive-asset test.[[1]](#source-1)
- EU **PRIIPs** rules generally require a Key Information Document (KID) before
  a packaged retail investment product is made available to a retail
  investor.[[4]](#source-4) Many U.S.-domiciled ETFs do not publish one.

This is often called the "PFIC vs. MiFID II" problem, but that shorthand is
imprecise. The KID requirement comes from the PRIIPs Regulation, not MiFID II.
MiFID II is relevant because it defines retail and professional client status,
including when an investor may request professional treatment.[[5]](#source-5)

## The Conflict

| Investment                        | U.S. federal issue                                                                            | EU retail-access issue                                                                               |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| U.S.-domiciled ETF                | Generally not a PFIC because it is not a foreign corporation                                  | A broker generally cannot make it available to an EU retail investor without the required PRIIPs KID |
| EU-domiciled ETF or mutual fund   | Often a PFIC because the fund is a foreign corporation dominated by passive income and assets | Commonly available with a KID                                                                        |
| Individual non-U.S. company share | Must be tested under the PFIC income and asset rules; it is not automatically safe            | A direct share is generally not a PRIIP merely because it is foreign                                 |
| Individual U.S. company share     | Not a PFIC                                                                                    | A direct share is generally outside the packaged-product KID problem                                 |

The rules regulate different things. PFIC status is a U.S. tax classification of
a **foreign corporation**. PRIIPs regulates how a **packaged product** is made
available to an EU retail investor. Neither rule categorically prohibits owning
the affected investment.

## PFIC Consequences

The default Section 1291 regime does more than apply ordinary income tax. An
excess distribution or gain on disposition is allocated across the holding
period; amounts assigned to prior PFIC years are charged using the highest tax
rate for each year, plus underpayment interest.[[2]](#source-2)

Form 8621 may be required for each PFIC held directly or indirectly. The current
IRS instructions also describe exceptions and two important alternative
regimes: a Qualified Electing Fund (QEF) election and, for eligible marketable
stock, a mark-to-market election.[[3]](#source-3) These alternatives have timing,
documentation, and transition rules, so they are not automatic cures.

The blanket advice to avoid every foreign fund also misses account-level rules.
For example, the IRS instructions state that a U.S. person is not treated as a
PFIC shareholder for stock held through certain U.S. tax-exempt organizations
or accounts, including an individual retirement plan or annuity as defined in
Internal Revenue Code Section 7701(a)(37).[[3]](#source-3) Foreign pensions and
the interaction with an applicable tax treaty require separate analysis.

## PRIIPs and Professional-Client Status

Under PRIIPs, the product manufacturer must prepare and publish the KID before a
PRIIP is made available to retail investors, and the seller or adviser must
provide it in good time before the investor is bound.[[4]](#source-4) In
practice, an EU broker may therefore allow an existing U.S. ETF position to be
held or sold while blocking new retail purchases. The broker's country, product
classification, and own compliance policy still matter.

MiFID II permits a retail client to request elective professional status, but a
€500,000 portfolio alone is insufficient. The investment firm must assess the
client and at least two of these three criteria must be satisfied:

1. An average of 10 significant transactions per quarter over the previous four
   quarters in the relevant market.
1. A financial-instrument portfolio, including cash deposits, exceeding
   €500,000.
1. At least one year of relevant professional experience in the financial
   sector.

The client must request the status in writing, and the firm must warn in writing
about protections and investor-compensation rights that may be lost.[[5]](#source-5)
A firm is not required to approve the request.

## Planning Takeaways

- Confirm the policy of each broker for the destination country and account type
  before changing residence; provider policy is not the same as a legal rule.
- Record each fund's legal domicile. Trading currency, exchange, index, or brand
  does not establish whether the issuer is a U.S. or foreign corporation.
- Do not assume a foreign operating company is PFIC-free. Apply the statutory
  income and asset tests, especially for cash-rich companies and holding
  companies.
- Before buying a foreign fund, have a U.S. cross-border tax professional model
  the default Section 1291 treatment and determine whether QEF or mark-to-market
  treatment is both available and practical.
- Treat professional-client status as a regulated classification with reduced
  protections, not merely a workaround for fund access.
- Analyze the destination country's income, wealth, disclosure, and estate rules
  separately; solving PFIC and PRIIPs access does not settle local taxation.

## Review of *The US Expat Money Guide*

The guide is useful as a plain-language introduction to the cross-border fund
problem, particularly its warning to review brokerage policies before moving.
Its legal and planning claims need these qualifications:

- The KID restriction is attributed to MiFID II, but PRIIPs is the direct legal
  source.
- The guide says individual foreign stocks do not trigger PFIC treatment. Any
  foreign corporation can be a PFIC if it meets a statutory test.
- The guide reduces elective professional status to a €500,000 portfolio plus
  experience. MiFID II requires a firm assessment and at least two of three
  criteria, followed by a written opt-up procedure.
- The statement that PFIC taxation means the highest ordinary rate plus interest
  describes the default Section 1291 mechanics only incompletely; QEF and
  mark-to-market regimes, holding-period allocation, exceptions, and account
  context matter.
- References to particular brokers, account availability, and "offshore"
  structures are provider-specific and can change. They should be verified
  directly and do not remove U.S. or local reporting obligations.
