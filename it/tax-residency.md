As of June 19, 2026, the core domestic rule is TUIR Article 2. For Italian
income-tax purposes, an individual is resident if, for the greater part of the
tax year, counting fractions of days, at least one of these is true: [[1]](#source-1)

- The person has civil-code residence in Italy.
- The person has domicile in Italy. For this rule, domicile means the place
  where the person's personal and family relationships principally develop.
- The person is physically present in Italy.
- Separately, registration for the greater part of the tax year in the resident
  population registry, the `anagrafe`, creates a rebuttable presumption of tax
  residence.

## Practical Non-Residency Screen

Holding a [permesso di soggiorno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permesso_di_soggiorno)
does not by itself settle tax residence. It is immigration status, not the whole
income-tax residency test. If the goal is to avoid Italian tax residency, the
practical screen is:

- Keep Italy presence clearly below 183 days in the tax year. Under the current
  domestic rule, fractions of days count, so do not model this as exact
  24-hour blocks.
- Do not register in the `anagrafe` unless you intend to become resident or have
  taken advice that the registration is required for your plan. Registration for
  most of the year puts the burden on you to rebut residency.
- Keep the ordinary home, spouse or partner, close family life, doctors,
  memberships, social life, and daily routine centered outside Italy. The
  current domicile definition is focused on personal and family relationships,
  not just business or bank accounts.
- Keep evidence: travel calendar, entry and exit records, boarding passes,
  accommodation records, leases, utility bills, medical records, and evidence of
  daily life outside Italy.
- Get professional advice before combining a long Italian stay with remote work,
  family relocation, an Italian lease, or local registration.

## Treaty Tie-Breaker

If Italy and the United States both treat you as resident under their domestic
rules, Article 4 of the U.S.-Italy income-tax treaty supplies tie-breakers:
permanent home, center of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality, and then
competent-authority agreement if needed. [[2]](#source-2)

That treaty analysis is a fallback for dual-residence cases, not a substitute
for managing the Italian domestic facts. Treat it as a documented tax position
to discuss with an expat tax adviser, especially if the facts include a
long-term Italian home or family presence in Italy.
